Express Shopper

Employer Google
Role UX Design Intern
Time 3 months
Tools Sketch, InVision

In summer 2018, I interned in Google Shopping UX team as a UX Designer working on Express Shopper. Express Shopper is a mobile app designed by Google that helps store associates fulfill orders from Google Express. After consumers place orders on Express, store associates use Express Shopper to collect items in the stores and have the packages delivered. As the sole designer for the project, I designed and launched the camera-based scanning feature for Express Shopper in 3 months.

Field Study

I observed and interviewed users and stakeholders in the field, their actual working context, to understand their pain points.

Usability Test

When I had my design in higher fidelity, I created clickable prototypes and conducted usability testing in the field.

Design Spec

After finalizing my design based on the testing, I created design spec and collaborated with developers to implement the feature.

Design Process

The Problem

In order to make sure store associates are processing the correct items being ordered, they are required to scan the item barcode during the fulfillment process. Currently, they use handheld bluetooth scanners to do so. However, in the field studies where I went to 3 merchant stores, interviewed 3 store managers, and shadowed 5 store associates in the fulfillment process, I found out that there are many usability issues with the bluetooth scanners:

High depreciation

The bluetooth scanners get broken easily. 25% of them need to be replaced every quarter, which is an enormous operation cost that can potential be reduced.

Unstable connection

Store associates often spend time reconnecting the scanner and the phone because of the unstable bluetooth while they are evaluated by speed of processing orders.

Additional burden

The scanners are extra hassels for store associates to charge and to carry around while their hands are often busy carrying the items they are picking.

As a result, the idea is to enable store associates to scan item barcode directly with the phone's camera given that: Google has barcode scanning API that our team can take advantage of, and that We want to reduce the amount of devices that the store associates have to carry.

In addition to field studies, I also conducted stakeholder interviews to dig deeper into the problem by taking perspectives from operation managers who develop standard process, monitor quality, and create training materials. Synthesizing findings from the field studies and stakeholder interviews, I defined 3 key How Might We statements:

How might we provide easy and quick access to the camera scanner for store associates?
How might we enable store associates to use the camera scanner without others' help or additional training?
How might we design the scanner that fits the need of merchants with different order volumes?

Wireframe and User Flow

My first design idea was to provide an entry to the camera scanner from both the list page and the item page. This is because I noticed that in different stores, store associates process differently.

In merchant stores like Costco, because there are not a lot of variations (e.g. different flavors, different sizes) of a same product, most store associates can identify the item to pick without going into the item page.

On the other hand, in stores like Smart and Final, there are more variations of a certain product. For example, a cookie of a brand can have up to 5 different flavors. As a result, store associates tend to go into item detail page to check the item information before picking it.

In order to serve the needs of the 2 different types of stores, I proposed to provide an entry to the camera scanner from both the list page and the item page. I further proposed different flow for the 2 different use cases:

Scan from list

When scanning from the list page, if the barcode scanned doesn't match any the item in the list, it turns off the camera and goes back to the list page.

Scan from item page

When scanning from the item page, if the barcode scanned doesn't match the item, it stays at the camera view with the alert highlighting a unique identifier for the item.

I designed the second flow this way by assuming that the most frequent case that the barcode scanned doesn't match the item happens when there are 2 (or more) very similar items that are next to each other. However, this idea was pushed back by the developer because I wasn't able to prove that this is the most frequent case and he wants to keep the flow unify and simple.

I decided to go with unified flow not only because of the developer's opinion, but also due to my learning that keeping the camera on will consume more battery life - and this is not good to the store associates in that currently there's no standard process of charging the phone and that they don't want to be forced to stop working because the phone is running out of battery.

Design Variations

After finalizing the flow, I explored many variations of the design of the camera trigger and the scanner UI.



Usability Testing

In order to find out what version works best in the actual working context, I conducted 6 usability testings in 5 stores with 3 different prototypes. Here are the key findings:

  • Overall reaction
    All store associates are positive about the idea of replacing bluetooth scanners with camera-based scanning

  • Discovery of camera trigger
    Nearly all store associates have no difficulties triggering the camera, no matter which option is given

  • Learning of scanning barcode with camera - Half of store associates spent longer time scanning items using the scanner without a frame

  • User control of camera focus
    4 out of 6 store associates tried tapping on the screen when the camera doesn’t focus on the barcode Some operator kept moving the phone back and forth while the camera takes some time to focus

During the usability tests, I found out the answers to my main concerns:

  • Consistency of camera triggers
    Q: Is It important to keep the buttons consistent across list page and item page?
    A: 6 No, store associates were able to find the buttons no matter they are consistent across the 2 pages or not
    Decision: Customize the camera triggers for list page and item item

  • Size of camera trigger at list page
    Q: Will store associates dislike Extended FAB because with the alert, they take up too much space and cover the list?
    A: Only 1 user talked about the concern, but the rest didn't bring it up during the test
    Decision: Go with extended FAB since it is the easiest to understand, find, and tap, but deduce the wording of the alert to make it smaller and thus less interruptive

  • Scanner UI
    Q: Is it better to have a scanner without a frame or with a frame?
    A: Half of users spent longer time scanning with the scanner without a frame
    Decision: Go with scanner with a frame

The Solution


Before Scanning

Scanning can be access from both list page and item page. The extended FAB with the combination of icon and wording is easy to find, easy to understand, and easy to tap.

During Scanning

A frame and a short instruction are provided so that store associates have an idea about how and where to locate the barcode of an item.

After Scanning

A confirmation dialog pops up together with the sound and vibration to let store associates know that they are picking the correct/incorrect items.

Outcome


I finalized my design based on the findings from the usability testing. I then collaborated with the developer to implement the feature by providing design spec and redlines. The feature is planned to be completed by the end of Q3 and will be launched by the end of the year (2018), enabling dozens of merchant stores and hundreds of store associates to scan item barcode with the camera scanner.
